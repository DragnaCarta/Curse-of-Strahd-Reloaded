# DM Guide — Keeping the Campaign on Track
**For a 16-session, once-weekly, 3–4 hour campaign**

> This guide answers the single hardest question in running Curse of Strahd: **how do you let the party explore freely while making sure a 4-month campaign actually finishes the story?**

---

## The Core Philosophy

You are not railroading the party. You are running a **living world with a ticking clock**.

Strahd's plan is real. It has a timeline. The Grand Conjunction has a deadline. The party does not have to do anything in any particular order — but every session they spend going sideways is a session where Strahd's preparations advance. The pressure is diegetic: it comes from the story, not from you forcing them down a path.

**The party has freedom. The story has gravity.** Your job is to keep the gravity working.

---

## The Session Budget

**Each session is 3–4 hours.** Budget accordingly:

| Phase | Time | What Happens |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Opening (recap + hook) | 15–20 min | Read your `recap.md` from last session; deliver the opening hook from `plan.md` |
| Scene 1 | 45–60 min | Primary encounter or major NPC interaction |
| Scene 2 | 45–60 min | Second beat — complication, revelation, or combat |
| Scene 3 | 30–45 min | Climax or pivot — the thing they'll remember |
| **Level-Up** | **15–20 min** | **Dedicated level-up time — see below** |

**If you're running long on Scene 1:** Cut Scene 2, not Scene 3. The level-up block is non-negotiable — guard it every session.

**If the party goes sideways in Scene 1:** This is normal. Let them explore for 20 minutes, then have an NPC redirect: a raven delivers a message, Muriel appears with news, a scream from the direction they were meant to go. Don't punish exploration — redirect it.

---

## Level-Up Block (End of Every Session — 15–20 Minutes)

Every session ends with a dedicated level-up. Do not skip this, do not rush it, and do not let it bleed into next session's opening. It is the tangible reward for the session's work.

### How to Run It Well

**1. Announce it in-world, not out-of-world.**  
Don't just say "ok everyone level up." Frame it as a rest or a narrative beat:
> *"You make camp in the shadow of Argynvostholt's walls. The fire is small but warm. For the first time tonight, no one is speaking. You can feel — something has shifted."*

Then: "Everyone take a level."

**2. Give each player 5 minutes, not 15.**  
- New HP (roll or take average — decide as a group before Session 1 which method you're using)
- New features written down or noted in their character sheet app
- Any new spells chosen

If someone has a complicated level-up (e.g., multiclass, subclass choice at this level): let them step away for 5 minutes and handle it while you run the closing hook conversation with the others. Don't hold the table.

**3. Narrate the milestone.**  
After the mechanical level-up, briefly name what they earned it for:
> *"You've driven Volenta from St. Andral's. The bones are back in the altar. The ward is up. Forty people are safer tonight because of what you did."*

One sentence. It doesn't need to be long. It just needs to connect the number on the sheet to something that happened.

**4. End on the next hook, not the last fight.**  
After level-up, give them a single forward-facing sentence — something to chew on until next week:
> *"As you settle in, Muriel perches on the windowsill in raven form. She drops a folded note and flies off before you can ask. The note has one word on it: 'Winery.'"*

Then stop. Don't explain. Don't resolve it. Let it sit for a week.

### When to Award 2 Levels
See `level_scaling.md` for the full list, but the short version: award 2 levels when a **major chapter milestone** is completed — escaping Death House, completing the Ravenloft heist, empowering the Sunsword. On those sessions, extend the level-up block to 25–30 minutes. Two levels means two rounds of HP, features, and spell choices. Worth the time.

### If You Run Out of Time Before the Level-Up Block
This happens. End the scene at a natural pause (don't cut mid-combat), do a 5-minute abridged version:
- "Everyone gains a level, take your HP, we'll handle spell picks over text before next session"
- Note it in `recap.md` so you don't forget what was awarded

Never skip the level entirely. The party's sense of progress is a pacing tool — it tells them the session mattered.

## The Three Tools for Staying on Track

### 1. The Pressing Deadline
Every act has a visible, ticking threat that forces the party forward:

| Act | Deadline | What Happens If Missed |
|-----|---------|----------------------|
| Act I | Strahd's next visit to Barovia village | Ireena is taken; Ismark is killed |
| Act II | St. Andral's Feast (Neyavr 7) | Volenta massacres the congregation |
| Act II | Festival of the Blazing Sun | Vallaki erupts; Wachter loses leverage |
| Act III | Wintersplinter completes | Winery is destroyed; Martikovs lose the gems |
| Act III | Full moon (Neyavr 14) | Heist window closes (Ravenloft is more dangerous after) |
| Act IV | Grand Conjunction begins | Arabelle is taken; the Fanes must be consecrated immediately |

**Use these deadlines openly.** The party should always know what happens if they dally. Information is not railroading; it's stakes.

### 2. The Friendly NPC Redirect
Every major arc has an NPC who can naturally pull the party back on track:

| Who | How They Help |
|-----|-------------|
| **Muriel Vinshaw** | Wereraven who can appear almost anywhere delivering urgent news (Keepers of the Feather have eyes everywhere) |
| **Madam Eva** | Her reading provides a roadmap the party returns to; "the cards say the sword awaits, but the ward must hold first" |
| **Ismark** | Can send letters via raven; his concern for Ireena creates natural urgency |
| **Davian Martikov** | Requests to the party create pull toward the winery and Yester Hill |
| **Ezmerelda** | Once met, she is a useful "what should we do next?" NPC who can prioritize objectives |

When the party is wandering: have one of these NPCs appear with specific, urgent information. Not a vague rumor — a specific request with a named consequence.

### 3. Strahd's Escalating Pressure
Strahd does not wait. Between sessions, his plans advance:

| Session Range | Strahd Behavior | What the Party Sees |
|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1–4 | Gentleman; curious | He appears once; watches; does not fight |
| 5–8 | Gentleman cooling; starting to test | One uninvited visit; a patrol of spawn near the inn |
| 9–11 | Tyrant emerging (post-Beacon) | Nightly harassment during long rests; spawn attack at dawn |
| 12–14 | Full Tyrant | Active interference; Rahadin appears with a message |
| 15–16 | Monster | Direct assault; no more games |

**Between sessions:** If the party spent a session not advancing the main plot, describe one consequence of Strahd's progress at the *opening* of the next session. A missing Barovian. A burned farmhouse. A new restriction at the town gate. The world reacts. This is not punishment — it is a reminder that time passes.

## The "Off-Script" Decision Tree

When the party goes off-script, run through this:

```
Is what they're doing fun and interesting?
  ├─ YES → Let it play out for 20–30 minutes; watch for a natural re-entry point
  └─ NO  → Redirect immediately (see: Friendly NPC Redirect above)

Will it take more than one session to resolve?
  ├─ YES → Is it more interesting than the main plot?
  │          ├─ YES → Fold it into the main plot; find the connection
  │          └─ NO  → NPC appears with urgent news; party can revisit this later
  └─ NO  → Let it happen; they'll be back on track by end of session

Does it break a key story beat?
  (e.g., party kills Ireena, destroys the Sunsword, burns Wachterhaus before the coup)
  └─ Adjust, don't railroad: find the version of the story that uses what they did
     The party killed Ireena? Strahd goes berserk, skips to full Monster phase immediately.
     They destroyed the Sunsword hilt? Sergei can empower a different silver weapon — it's weaker, but it works.
```

## Using Your Files Effectively

### Before Every Session (15 minutes of prep)
1. Open this week's **`plan.md`** — review the session goal, key decisions to watch, and contingency notes
2. Read the last session's **`recap.md`** — find the dangling threads and pick one to open on
3. Look at **`plot.md`** — identify Scene 1, Scene 2, Scene 3; know what you're driving toward
4. Check the relevant NPC files for any NPCs appearing this session — know their voice

### During the Session
- Keep **`notes.md`** open and jot bullet points as things happen — don't try to write full sentences
- Mark decisions in **`notes.md`** under "Key Decisions" — you'll want these for `recap.md`
- If the party does something unexpected that matters: note it under "Dangling Threads"

### After Every Session (15 minutes)
- Fill in **`recap.md`** while it's fresh — even just bullet points
- Update `Items/README.md` party inventory section
- Review next session's `plan.md` and add notes from this session's surprises

## The "Fun First" Rule

The plot exists to serve the fun, not the other way around.

If the party is deeply engaged in something off-script — if everyone is leaning forward, talking over each other, genuinely excited — **do not redirect them.** Let it run. You can advance the clock without penalizing the party: describe consequences of the delay at the next session's opening, but do not cut the fun short to stay on the railroad.

The goal of the 16-session structure is to reach the final battle with a party that has **earned** their victory — by building relationships, gathering tools, making choices that matter. If they did that in a way you didn't plan, the final battle is still earned. It might be better.

## Common Situations and How to Handle Them

### "The party refuses to go to Vallaki."
Let them sit in Barovia village for one session. Have Strahd visit Ireena while they're there. She survives (he doesn't want to damage her — yet), but she is shaken. The urgency of getting her to safety becomes visceral, not abstract.

### "The party wants to assault Castle Ravenloft immediately."
Let them try the gates. Rahadin is there. He does not fight them immediately — he looks at them the way you look at children playing with fire, and says, "The Count is not receiving visitors who have not been invited. I suggest you come back when you have something worth his time." If they press: he fights, and they lose. But he does not kill them. He lets them go. "Come back when you have earned an audience."

### "The party wants to ally with Lady Wachter but also keep the Baron."
Can't have both. Wachter makes this clear. Push the party to commit: "You need a decision by Neyavr 7. Delaying is choosing the Baron."

### "The party killed an NPC who was supposed to be important later."
- **Ismark killed:** Ireena has no one to anchor her to Barovia; she becomes the party's responsibility directly, which is more intense, not less. Use it.
- **Madam Eva killed:** The reading already happened. The Tarokka locations are set. What's lost is a future guidance source — have her reading replay in Ireena's dreams instead.
- **Van Richten killed:** Ezmerelda takes over his role. She knows everything he knew. She is angrier about it.
- **Ireena killed:** Strahd's Tyrant phase begins immediately (he is furious, not grief-stricken — he is beyond grief). The campaign is now a war, not a rescue. It's harder and more brutal. It can still be completed.

### "A player wants to accept a dark gift from the Amber Temple."
Let them. Track the corruption privately. Present each escalation as a neutral fact ("your hands look slightly pale today — you hadn't noticed before"). Don't moralize; let the story moralize.

### "The party is having so much fun in Vallaki they don't want to leave."
Winery news arrives: the Martikovs have sent a raven. The winery is under siege. If the party doesn't come within two days, the vineyard fails, and Vallaki's wine supply (and the Martikovs' gem) is gone. Two days is enough time to finish what they're doing. It's not enough to keep dawdling.

## Session Opening Hooks (When You're Stuck)

If you sit down to a session and aren't sure how to open it:

1. **Start mid-action.** "You're halfway down the road when you hear — ahead, not behind — the sound of something large moving through the trees."
2. **Start with an NPC.** Muriel appears at the inn window in raven form. She has a message. Read it.
3. **Start with a consequence.** "The town crier in Vallaki has new orders on the board this morning. You read: 'By order of the Burgomaster — all travel west of the gates is suspended until further notice.'"
4. **Start with Strahd.** A letter delivered by bat, sealed with his signet. Formal. Addressed by name to one specific party member. He has been paying attention.
5. **Start with a question.** Ask each player: "What did your character do last night while the others were sleeping?" This generates hooks from the party itself.

## The Final Session Checklist

Before Session 16, confirm the party has:
- [ ] The Sunsword (empowered)
- [ ] The Holy Symbol of Ravenkind
- [ ] All three Fanes consecrated (or know they haven't and understand the consequence)
- [ ] At least 3 major allies confirmed for the war council
- [ ] The Heart of Sorrow identified as the primary first target

If any of these are missing: Session 15 can be extended to "clean them up." The final session should start at the war council, not still assembling prerequisites.

**The final battle should feel inevitable in the best possible way.** The party has been building to this for four months. Trust the work they put in. Trust the work you put in. Give Strahd every mechanical advantage you wrote into his stat block — and let the Sunsword do what it was made to do.

Let the fog lift on its own.
